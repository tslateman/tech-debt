"CLARIFYING TECHNICAL DEBT"

https://davidedwards.io/blog/2024/12/29/clarifying-technical-debt

When you say "tech debt" do you include quality issues?

clumping quality issues into the nebulous bucket of technical debt is that organizations outside of software engineering, product management included, are free to interpret as they wish. It is not uncommon for people to equate technical debt with "the engineers made a bunch of mistakes or bad decisions"

Were they necessary shortcuts, even mandated by non-engineers? Do we need new language?

I would strongly encourage software organizations to eliminate technical debt from their vocabulary–particularly when it comes to planning–in exchange for clearly articulated quality improvements connected to business outcomes.

Noble, idealistic concept. Good luck. My attempts at these conversations have resulted in non-technical folks getting impatient, eyes glazing over, and people walking away with the wrong understanding and dubious conclusions.

"STOP SAYING "TECHNICAL DEBT""

https://stackoverflow.blog/2023/12/27/stop-saying-technical-debt/

Optimistic. Fine, let's go ahead and set the Platonic ideal.

How do we minimize maintenance load growth? With good code stewardship practices. We rarely reward, recognize, or teach code stewardship the way that we do feature development skills. But code stewardship skills—documenting systems, recovering context from code, and designing for future changes—make the difference between a team that hums along for a decade or more and a team that repeatedly mires itself in declarations of code bankruptcy, rewrites, and despair.

If only. Surprise! It goes further.

The Holy Grail? Negative maintenance load growth: the kind of growth that makes our code more maintainable over time instead of less. The Grail requires even more of the team than a healthy quotidian code stewardship routine. It requires us to look at individual maintenance tasks, track their origins, and address those problems at the source. These chores, backed by empirical evidence, give us something concrete to discuss in meetings about tech debt.

What a dream. Maybe one day. I strive for this, but I fear the bar is too high.

"DEVELOPER PHILOSOPHY"

https://qntm.org/devphilo

Beware compounding complexity.

Warning signs to watch for: compounding technical debt. Increasing difficulty in making seemingly simple changes to code. Difficulty in documenting/commenting code. Difficulty in onboarding new developers. Dwindling numbers of people who know how particular areas of the codebase actually work. Bugs nobody understands.

Compounding complexity must be fought at every turn. Alternate between phases of expansion (new features) and consolidation.

See also: Beware the complexity merchants

"TECH DEBT METAPHOR MAXIMALISM"

https://apenwarr.ca/log/20230605

Let's add more terminology: interest rate, total debt, and tech assets.

What's the interest rate on your technical debt? The ongoing extra work you need to do, because of the debt. For example, maybe you have to maintain two incompatible versions of your app while you finish migrating. (If you're doing a gradual migration, that is; most big-bang migrations fail.)

But actually... is debt always bad? Look at the other side of the coin.

Tech debt is any code you have that you have to repeatedly touch as you work on new or existing features. In the financial world, that sounds like an asset, not a debt, right? The code that's reused most often is your best code.

This one is really philosophical, a good read. Debt is ok as long as the interest rate is reasonable.

if your interest-to-income ratio is 0.3:1, that sounds about right. You're investing about 30% of your time into maintenance. I bet that's about how much homeowners spend on maintenance. Anything more than that is pretty sketchy.

"THE HUMAN COST OF TECH DEBT"

https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/human-cost-tech-debt-erik-dietrich

Hmm, I never considered the human cost of tech debt.

the longer I stick around, the more I look like an archaeologist rather than a programmer. I can't deliver any new functionality until I've investigated, understood, translated, made sense of, and interpreted all of the legacy code in and around the new feature.

Makes sense. Eventually new features become impossible.

Developers working in high tech debt situations chronically underestimate the complexity of feature requests, and miss deadlines. Business stakeholders get understandably frustrated when what sounds like a simple request winds up taking a team a month. Developers work long hours and still wind up looking incompetent, and everyone involved in the app ecosystem becomes miserable. People quit, and many of those that remain do so only out of fear of a job search and a resulting pay cut.

The solution: make tech debt payments a real part of the process.

"THE ULTIMATE ENGINEERING LEADER'S GUIDE TO TECHNICAL DEBT"

https://leaddev.com/technical-debt-tech-strategy/ultimate-engineering-leaders-guide-technical-debt

A great reference for thinking of debt in categories and quadrants.

Fowler's Technical Debt Quadrant:

  • Reckless & Deliberate: "We don't have time for design"
  • Prudent & Deliberate: "We must ship now and deal with consequences"
  • Reckless & Inadvertent: "What's layering?"
  • Prudent & Inadvertent: "Now we know how we should have done it"

Don't fall into the trap that all debt is bad. Prudent debt can be acceptable.

Aggressively pursuing zero tech debt is also problematic. This is the pursuit of perfection at the expense of product success. Think of that hackathon product that was scrappy and exciting at the start. Six months later, it is a monument to over-engineering...

"FUNCTIONAL DEBT VS. TECHNICAL DEBT"

https://wasp-lang.dev/blog/2023/07/10/gajdukov-7-takeaways-on-technical-debt

What is functional debt?

Product or UX-related debt is incurred when shortcuts are taken in the product development process, such as skipping user research or not fully understanding user needs.

The post goes on to paraphrase ProductPlan's article on technical debt:

Making a conscious decision to release a product that is faster but has messier code and less functionality would be taking on technical debt. Choosing to skip proper user testing to meet a launch date would be functional debt.

Ah, makes sense to categorize it separately.

"HOW TO MEASURE TECHNICAL DEBT"

https://unqork.com/resource-center/guides/how-to-measure-technical-debt-a-guide-for-it-stakeholders/

How do we put a number on it? From McKinsey's Tech Debt Ratio to using Jira ticket tagging and tracking effort over time.

Measurement approaches include:

  • Interest Rate Approximation: (Maintenance Hours / Total Development Hours) × (% Attributed to Technical Debt) × 100
  • Tracking Time Spent: Log hours specifically addressing technical debt
  • Code Quality Metrics: Static analysis tools, cyclomatic complexity, code coverage

"MANAGING TECHNICAL DEBT IN A MICROSERVICE ARCHITECTURE"

https://www.infoq.com/articles/managing-technical-debt-microservices/

Microservices bring their own unique debt challenges:

In a microservice architecture, technical debt can accumulate not just within individual services, but also in the interactions between services, in the deployment infrastructure, and in the overall system architecture.

The article discusses strategies specific to microservices: service boundaries, API versioning, distributed tracing, and the importance of service ownership.
